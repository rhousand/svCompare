# Sailboat Buyer's Question Guide

Use this to evaluate and compare any boat. Score each section as you go — patterns of red flags matter more than any single issue.

---

## Ownership & History

**1. How many owners has the boat had?**
- **Good:** 1–2 owners. Fewer owners generally means more consistent maintenance culture.
- **Avoid:** 3+ owners in a short period, or a history of quick resales — often signals recurring problems or disappointment.

**2. Has it ever been in a charter fleet? If so, for how many seasons?**
- **Good:** Never chartered, or private use only.
- **Caution:** 1–2 charter seasons with documented professional maintenance isn't a dealbreaker — charter companies often service boats on a strict schedule.
- **Avoid:** 3+ charter seasons, especially with a bareboat operation. Guests are hard on boats. Expect worn upholstery, stressed rigging, abused engines, and deferred cosmetic repairs. Discount should reflect this.

**3. Is there a complete maintenance log?**
- **Good:** Logbook or digital record showing regular oil changes, impeller replacements, rigging checks, haulouts, etc. This signals an owner who cared.
- **Avoid:** "I just did stuff as needed" with nothing to show for it. No records = no accountability. Budget for a full systems audit.

**4. Has the boat ever run aground or been in a collision? Any insurance claims?**
- **Good:** Clean history with no claims.
- **Caution:** A soft grounding in sand with documented inspection is not necessarily serious.
- **Avoid:** Any collision or hard grounding with no subsequent survey. Keel damage, rudder damage, and hull stress fractures can be hidden cosmetically. Always get an independent survey regardless of what the seller says.

## Engine & Mechanical

**5. What are the current engine hours?**
- **Good:** Under 1,000 hours for a boat 5–7 years old (~100–150 hrs/year for light use). Well-maintained diesels can go 3,000–5,000+ hours but cost more to maintain as they age.
- **Caution:** 1,500–2,500 hours — not a dealbreaker but factor in upcoming service costs.
- **Avoid:** Very low hours (under 200) on a boat that is several years old. Infrequently run diesels develop problems from sitting. Ask why it hasn't been used.

**6. When was the last engine service, and what was done?**
- **Good:** Full service within the last 12 months or 100 hours — oil, filters, impeller, belts, zincs.
- **Avoid:** "It runs fine" with no documented recent service. Budget $500–$1,500 for a full diesel service immediately if records are absent.

**7. Is the diesel impeller, belts, and heat exchanger current?**
- **Good:** Impeller replaced within the last season. Belt and heat exchanger inspected.
- **Avoid:** Unknown impeller history — a failed impeller destroys the heat exchanger and can cause the engine to overheat in minutes. This is a cheap part (~$30) with expensive consequences if neglected.

**8. What is the condition of the standing and running rigging? When was it last replaced?**
- **Good:** Standing rigging (shrouds, stays) replaced within 10 years or 10,000 nm. Running rigging soft and pliable, not stiff or fuzzy.
- **Avoid:** Original rigging on a boat over 8–10 years old with no replacement. Rigging failure at sea is catastrophic. Budget $3,000–$8,000+ for a full re-rig if it's due.

## Sails & Rig

**9. What year are the sails from? Are they original?**
- **Good:** Sails replaced or lightly used — original sails with fewer than 500 hours still have life. Sails from a previous owner's upgrade are a bonus.
- **Caution:** Original sails are fine if the boat hasn't been sailed hard. UV damage and delamination are the killers — inspect in person.
- **Avoid:** Sails that are stiff, delaminating (layers separating), have heavy UV strip fading, or numerous patches. New offshore-quality sails for a 40-footer run $8,000–$20,000+.

**10. What sail inventory is included?**
- **Good:** Full main, furling headsail, and a bonus sail (code zero, asymmetric spinnaker, storm jib). More inventory = more value.
- **Avoid:** Just a main and furling jib with no extras. Fine for coastal sailing, but factor in what you'd need to add for your intended use.

**11. Has the mast ever been unstepped? When? Any issues found?**
- **Good:** Unstepped for a rigging replacement or inspection with no issues found.
- **Avoid:** Never unstepped on an older boat — wiring and rigging inside the mast degrades invisibly. Any vague answers about what was found during an unstepping deserves follow-up.

## Systems

**12. What is the battery bank configuration (capacity, age, type)?**
- **Good:** Lithium (LiFePO4) bank or AGM batteries less than 3 years old with 200+ Ah capacity. Proper battery monitor (Victron, etc.) installed.
- **Caution:** Older AGMs can appear to hold charge but fail under load. Ask to see a battery test.
- **Avoid:** Original batteries on a boat 5+ years old with no upgrades. Lead-acid batteries have a 3–5 year lifespan. Budget $1,500–$6,000+ for a proper replacement bank.

**13. Is there shore power? What is the inverter/charger setup?**
- **Good:** Working shore power inlet, GFCI-protected outlets, and a quality charger (Victron, Mastervolt). Inverter is a bonus for offshore use.
- **Avoid:** Corroded shore power connections or any DIY electrical work. Boat electrical fires are a leading cause of total losses. If the wiring looks messy, budget for a full inspection by a marine electrician.

**14. Is the head a holding tank system? Is it certified compliant?**
- **Good:** Y-valve system with a properly sized holding tank and macerator pump. Legally compliant for no-discharge zones.
- **Avoid:** Direct overboard discharge without a holding tank — illegal in most US waters. Retrofitting a compliant system costs $500–$2,000 and is disruptive.

**15. What is the age and condition of the standing rigging?**
*(Ask this separately from question 8 — sellers sometimes give inconsistent answers.)*
- **Good sign:** Consistent answer with question 8, showing the seller tracks maintenance carefully.
- **Red flag:** A different answer than question 8 — suggests the seller isn't maintaining or tracking the boat attentively.

## Survey & Hull Condition

**16. Has a recent marine survey been conducted? Can I see it?**
- **Good:** Survey within the last 2 years available for review.
- **Important:** The seller's survey protects the seller. Always commission your own independent survey — typically $20–$25 per foot.
- **Avoid:** No survey, or any resistance to letting you conduct one. No reputable seller blocks an independent survey. Walk away if they do.

**17. When was the boat last hauled? What bottom paint is on it?**
- **Good:** Hauled within the last 12–18 months. Fresh antifouling paint. Thru-hulls and zincs inspected.
- **Avoid:** Last hauled more than 2 years ago, or unknown. Expect fouled running gear, degraded zinc protection, and potentially barnacled thru-hulls. Budget $1,500–$3,000 for a haulout + bottom job.

**18. Is there any osmotic blistering or delamination on the hull?**
- **Good:** Clean hull with no blistering, or minor surface blisters already treated.
- **Caution:** Small surface blisters are cosmetic and treatable. Get details confirmed in the survey.
- **Avoid:** Deep structural blisters or delamination. A full osmotic treatment (barrier coat) can run $5,000–$15,000+ depending on severity and boat size.

**19. What is the condition of the rudder bearings and keel bolts?**
- **Good:** Rudder is firm with no slop when wiggled. Keel bolts inspected at last haulout with no weeping rust stains.
- **Avoid:** Any slop in the rudder — it will worsen and eventually fail. Rust staining around keel bolts is a serious red flag requiring immediate expert evaluation. Keel re-bedding is a major, expensive project.

## Electronics & Safety Equipment

**20. What electronics are included (chartplotter, AIS, VHF, autopilot)?**
- **Good:** Modern chartplotter (Garmin, Raymarine, B&G) with current charts, AIS transponder (not just receiver), working VHF, autopilot, and wind/speed/depth instruments. Radar is a strong bonus.
- **Caution:** Older electronics work fine but may lack integration. Factor in upgrade costs to your offer.
- **Avoid:** Non-functional electronics the seller "hasn't gotten around to fixing." Budget what it would cost to replace entirely — a full electronics suite for a 40-footer runs $5,000–$20,000+.

**21. Is there a windlass? Watermaker? Generator?**
- **Good:** Electric windlass in working condition is near-essential for shorthanded sailing. A watermaker and generator are serious value-adds for offshore or liveaboard use.
- **Avoid:** A windlass that "works sometimes" — chain jams at the worst moments. Verify function in person. A new windlass runs $800–$2,500 installed.

**22. What safety gear is included and what are the expiration dates?**
- **Good:** Life raft serviced within 3 years, flares current, EPIRBs registered and within hydrostatic test date, jacklines and tethers in good condition.
- **Avoid:** Expired safety gear doesn't protect you and costs real money to replace. A 4-person offshore life raft costs $3,000–$6,000+ to purchase or $400–$800 to service. Factor all expired gear into your negotiation.

## Transaction

**23. Is the price negotiable? Has the listing price been reduced?**
- **Good:** Seller acknowledges room to negotiate. A price reduction history shows motivation to sell.
- **Caution:** "Firm on price" isn't necessarily bad if the boat is priced fairly — verify against comparables first.
- **Strategy:** Always make a written offer 10–15% below asking after your survey. Use any survey findings as additional negotiating leverage.

**24. Where is the boat located, and what are the current slip costs?**
- **Good:** Boat is in the water, accessible for a sea trial, in a reputable marina.
- **Caution:** "In storage" or "on the hard" means you can't do a full sea trial until it's launched — factor in that cost (~$500–$1,500).
- **Avoid:** Boats stored in unfamiliar or remote locations far from professional resources. Buying sight-unseen is high risk.

**25. Is it available for a sea trial prior to purchase?**
- **Good:** Seller says yes without hesitation.
- **Avoid:** Any resistance to a sea trial. You must sail the boat before buying. A sea trial reveals engine performance under load, sail handling, instrument function, unusual sounds/smells, and how the boat feels. It is non-negotiable.
